Storython: Portugal's Lost Youth

Emigration is on the up, the country’s people search for their souls
Image: Dissolving Youth, by hom26, Creative Commons
Earlier this month, I acted as moderator and technical lead on a hackday in Berlin which brought together journalists, data scientists, activists and others to tell stories about migration in Europe. The three day event was part of the Slow Politics conference run by the Berliner Gazette. We enjoyed attendance by participants from across the world, with *media hackers* visiting from as far as Los Angeles and Portugal. My job was to present the topic, associated data, manage the media material, gather the technical pieces into order and help the team work together to its members' strengths. Our focus was Portugal, a south European country with a stream of emigrants leaving its borders in ever greater numbers, year on year. After some discussion, we opted to study that phenomenon. We began to piece together numbers, anecdotal evidence, imagery and words to give a sense of how the European crisis has affected the economics and psyche of the nation. During the event, we were also able to interview two people of Portuguese extraction who left the country and relayed the hopelessness they left their homeland with. The result was a longform piece which explains and evokes the melancholy of a place losing its people. We leaned on the following technologies: - **[Resonate](https://github.com/jplusplus/resonate2014)** rich article template - **[Datawrapper](https://datawrapper.de/)** charts from data - **[Timeline.js](http://timeline.knightlab.com/)** chronologies and data - **Google Sheets**, **Fusion Tables** and **Maps** - **iPad** and **iMovie** on-site video production There are so many directions an event like this can take, and it's easy for a comparatively large team (there were eight of us) to get lost in the ideas. Thankfully, we came out with a clear story, fresh material and a genuine contribution to the difficult history of Portugal in the context of a troubled European project.



Emigrants from Portugal have mostly dispersed across Europe in search of work and a better life.

Journalist Júlio Gomes, 38, left his home town of Porto in 2011 and now runs a magazine in Berlin where he lives with his youngest child.

He says he is "just one of these anonymous citizens who were forced to leave due to circumstances".

Neoliberal democracy has been able to expel more citizens in the last three years than the fascist colonial regime did in the sixties.

In a surprising address, Prime Minister Pedro Passos Coelho, told the country in 2011 that professionals, specifically teachers, struggling to find work in Portugal, should look outside of the country:

There are a lot of teachers that don't have a job in Portugal at the moment... They can look to the wider Portuguese language market for an alternative.

Many citizens were not expecting such discouraging language from a man entrusted to lead and champion the nation‘s fortunes.

In the first half of 2014, about 200 Portuguese doctors, similarly unable to find suitable work at home, followed the spirit of his suggestion.

In this way, Portugal has become a kind of recruiter‘s paradise, a phenomenon that started with nurses going abroad some time before. According to the Portuguese Medical Association, the country's health service will lose a total of 400 doctors by the end of the year.

Emigration of Portuguese citizens across the world is not a new phenomenon. The diaspora is now bigger than ever, with the country's young following well worn trails, albeit in greater numbers than ever before.
